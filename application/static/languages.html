What is morphology?

Morphology studies the elements that compose words: the morphemes. A morpheme is the smallest abstract unit that carries meaning. This unit can be isolated by segmentation. Eg. in English, the word 'player' can be segmented as follows: play+er. Cutting more would result in meaningless units, which means that 'play' and 'er' are morphemes. There are two kinds of morphemes: free morphemes and bound morphemes. On the one hand, free morphemes can stand alone with a specific meaning. On the other hand, bound morphemes cannot stand alone with meaning. Morphemes are composed of two distinct classes called (a) bases (or roots) and (b) affixes:

  • A base (or root) is an indecomposable morpheme, a free morpheme which has a concrete lexical meaning.
  • An affix is a part of a complex word, a bound morpheme with no lexical meaning but with a grammatical meaning in case of inflectional morphology. It occurs before or after a base. An affix that comes before a base is called a prefix. An affix that comes after a base is called a suffix.

Regarding the previous example, 'play' is the base (or root) and 'er' is an affix and precisely a suffix.

Genealogical classification

The genealogical classification is the most common way of classifying languages. Languages are classified according to their kinships. Therefore, genetically related languages are grouped in a language family.

  • German and Spanish belongs to the Indo-European language family.
  • Finnish and Hungarian are Uralic languages.
  • Turkish is a member of the Altaic family.

Typological classification

Another classification criterion is the structural similarity between all the languages that exist. Regarding the degree of internal complexity, there are 3 types of languages:

  • An analytic language is a language in which a word tends to consist of only one morpheme. This type of language has no inflection.
  • A synthetic language is a language in which a word tends to consist of more than one morpheme. This type of language is plenty of inflection, derivation and compounding.
  • A polysynthetic language is a very extremely synthetic language where words are very complex to the point of constituting a clause. This type of language has an extensive use of inflection, derivation and compounding.

Following this kind of classification, all of our five languages are synthetic.

Synthetic and polysynthetic languages can be divided into two types regarding the technique employed for combining morphemes: flectional and agglutinating languages.

  • The first property for agglutinating languages is that each morpheme expresses only one meaning unlike flectionals for which a morpheme can cumulate meanings.
  • Then, for agglutinating languages there is a clear-cut boundary between each morpheme contrary to flectionals where there is a fusion of morphemes which implies such close integration that morphological segmentation becomes impossible.
  • Finally, both types express their grammatical processes through prefixes and suffixes but flectional languages can also signal grammatical processes with the morpheme itself through an internal flection.

German and Spanish are both flectional languages. Finnish, Hungarian and Turkish are agglutinating languages. Moreover, in inflectional morphology, these 5 languages are predominantly suffixing.
